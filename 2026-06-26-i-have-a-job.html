I have a job

And it's a pretty cool one, too

The blog is now LinkedIn?

It's true what they say. I have a job. It's a pretty cool one, too.

Over the past few months, I've been interviewing with a company called Morning Consult (MC) to be an associate on their research and sampling strategy team. MC is a big market research company, meaning they conduct tens of thousands of surveys daily on behalf of clients to understand consumer sentiment around the world. The problem with their business model is that they conduct their surveys online, meaning that they're able to get many responses at relatively low cost but their samples tend not to be representative of the general population (or, at least, as representative as the General Social Survey or the American Trends Panel).1

So, what MC and its competitors are trying to do is perfect the science of non-probability samples. How do you twist a non-probability sample into a representative one? It could very well be a wild goose chase, but there's reason for hope. Some firms have seemingly been able to get non-probability sampling right. Of all pollsters, YouGov was the most accurate when predicting Trump's share of the popular vote in 2024. Meanwhile, Andy Gelman, a Columbia statistician, wrote a very famous paper in 2015, introducing a correction technique that used opt-in surveys of Xbox users to accurately forecast elections. If you want a better summary about the problems with online surveys, see this NYTimes article, which disses MC along the way for good reason.

When I was initially interviewing with MC, I was interviewing for an internal consultative role, in which I would be going around to their client-facing teams, saying "this is how you sample XYZ demographic group that ABC company believes is their consumer." Lame, and I didn't get that job (they went with an internal candidate).

They came back to me a week later, however, with a job that has the same title but different responsibilities. Instead of now being a consultant for others first, I'd now be an assistant to a bunch of polisci PhDs who are researching how the company can actually try to fix their sampling methodology to make it more representative. And that's pretty cool and exciting.

When do I start and all?

I'll start working virtually for MC on July 7th, with the expectation that I move to DC in either late July or early August. In moving, I'll now be near Conrad! So close in fact that our offices will be less than a ten minute walk from one another.

What else is going on?

Not too much. Pretty relaxed right now. I'll pick up a new book today, but I'm not sure which one yet. Will also try to read more about Gelman's polling corrections.

  1. The lack of representativeness of online surveys was one of the problems that I encountered with my senior thesis. Online surveys perform worse than other types of surveys across the board, but they perform especially badly in low-income countries. In India, for example, survey respondents are overwhelmingly well educated, high earners, and upper cast. Respondents also tend to be male and younger, but both of those things can be more easily corrected for using standard weighting techniques. That said, even more conventional sampling techniques tend to perform badly (or, at least, worse) in low income countries. ↩
