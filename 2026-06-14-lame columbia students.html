Yet more evidence Columbia students are lame

In which I resort to subways and shoddy statistics

Subways and Shoddy Statistics

It was only a matter of time before this blog became a data blog. Who am I to resist fate?

This weekend, I was casually going through NY Open Data (as I often do when I'm bored), and I came across a cool dataset. This dataset estimates how frequently people travel from certain subway stations to others, averaged by the hour of the day and month. So, I decided to try to figure out where Columbia students go. Of course, I don't want to overstate. I can't definitively say where Columbia students go, but I can estimate where subway riders who pick up the 1 from 116th are going. Take the title of this post as teasing clickbait, then. I don't mean to imply I can draw any definitive conclusions about Columbia students, but I at least try to manipulate my data to show that they are lame.

Ultimately, the best evidence I come up with is, in fact, the existence of this blog post itself.

The Weekends + Friday

First, let's start with the basics of how I manipulated these data. These data are from 2025, and I subset them to only include months during the academic year. First, I consider where people who take the train from 116th go on the weekends and on Friday.


Perhaps, unsurprisingly large transit hubs that the one stops at are the biggest destinations people go to. Things really tail off after Penn Station, though. Do people really spend that much time in the Upper West Side?

Trying to Focus on Columbia Students

Of course, the problem with doing the above is that they say nothing about Columbia students themselves. There are many people who take the one to and from 116th who aren't students—Mount Sinai employees, Morningside Heights residents, service workers, etc. So, in addition to just looking at where people go over the weekend, I also consider how traffic patterns differ between Thursday and Friday.

The intuition for why I do so is this: with classes off, Columbia students are footloose and free to go wherever they would like. That said, people who work still have to turn up, meaning that traffic patterns for everyone aside from Columbia students should be similar between the two days. The resulting difference between traffic patterns on Thursday and Friday may then be a good indication of where Columbia students, specifically, are going. To be sure, the assumption that I'm making about non-students' traffic patterns is strong. One problem with it is that people work from home more often on Fridays than Thursdays meaning that the change in hybrid workers' traveling habits will be reflected here. While I can obsess about issues like these, I can also just create a map. See below.


There are only two areas, so far as I can tell, that show a consistent increase in the number of people visiting them on Friday compared to Thursday. One is lower Manhattan north of Canal and below 14th Street, and the other is Rockaway. Other notable locations showing an increase in the number of people going to them include Inwood, Mets-Willets Point (but not Yankees' Stadium), and the aquarium.

I'm not sure what to make of the significant decline in people going to Penn Station, 42nd street, and 96th. Maybe it's people working from home? Maybe it's some artifact of the MTA's estimation methods? If you have any ideas, drop them in the comments.
